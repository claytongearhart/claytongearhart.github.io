Ionic & Covalent Bonds

There are many types of chemical bonds that hold molecules together, the two most basic types of bonds are ionic and covalent. In an ionic bond is a bond held together by differences in charge between atoms. In a covalent bond is a bond held together by sharing an electron between atoms.

Ionic Bonds

Ionic bonds form from atoms exchanging electrons in order to have a full shell. Ionic bonds form between metals and nonmetals because metals typically lack electron in their valence shell The outer most shell of an atom. while nonmetals, especially those on the far right of the periodic table have close to 8 electrons in their valence shell.

Covalent Bonds

Covalent bonds typically form from two atoms that are close to each other on the periodic table. Also, atoms with similar electronegativity The attraction electrons have to a given atom. are more likely to form covalent bonds. This is because electrons are equally attracted to both of them, so electrons are shared to create a full valence shell.

Naming

Ionic:

  • The cation is written first and the anion second.
  • The charges of the ions must cancel out, if the absolute value of one ion is not equal to another use subscripts to balance them.
  • Examples
    • \(\ce{Na^+ + Cl^- -> NaCl}\)
    • \(\ce{Ca^2+ + 2Cl^- -> CaCl_2}\)
    • \(\ce{3Mg^2+ + N_2^3- -> Mg_3N_2}\)


Covalent:

  • Covalent compounds are named using prefixes that indicate how many of the substance there are.
  • If there is only one of the first substance no prefix is used. The first material has the full name while the second material is the root, ending in -ide.
  • The prefixes listed respectively from one to ten are: mono, di, tri, tetra, penta, hexa, hepta, octa, nona, and deca.
  • Examples
    • Nitrogen dioxide - \(\ce{NO_2}\)
    • Chlorine trioxide - \(\ce{ClO_3}\)
    • Disodium Trioxide - \(\ce{Na_2O_3}\)
